# Episode Intelligence Node: Anxiety Ripple Effect (Part 4)
**Series:** Anxiety's Ripple Effect on Loved Ones (5-Part Series)
**Host:** Andrea Rice
**Guest:** Chan Maragh (Co-Founder, HGICG)
**Logic-Type:** Relational Communication / Boundaried Empathy / Cognitive Load Management

## 1. The Core Claim
Effective relational recovery requires a shift from "Attack/Defense" dynamics to **Shared Responsibility via Small-Batch Tasking.** Maintaining the sufferer's autonomy is critical; removing all responsibilities increases the "Lack of Control" spike that fuels the anxiety loop.

## 2. Branded Entities & Terms
- **The "I Feel" Pivot:** A communication protocol that replaces accusatory "You" statements with subjective emotional data to lower the sufferer's defensive filters.
- **Small-Batch Tasking:** The practice of breaking down overwhelming life-logistics (e.g., finances, chores) into micro-units (e.g., "2 bills today, 3 tomorrow") to prevent cognitive overload.
- **The Timing Protocol:** A strategic delay in delivering feedback or requests, ensuring the recipient is in a "Calm State" (e.g., after dinner vs. immediately after work) to maximize receptivity.
- **Autonomy Preservation:** The act of keeping the sufferer engaged in functional life tasks to prevent the anxiety-inducing sensation of "Total Loss of Control."

## 3. The Logic Chain (Fact Map)
1. **The Attack Filter:** High-anxiety states cause the brain to interpret neutral requests as personal attacks.
2. **Receptivity Windows:** Information processed during a panic or high-stress spike is discarded or distorted; feedback must be timed for "Low-Noise" periods.
3. **The Control Paradox:** While a partner may want to "take over" all chores to help, doing so strips the sufferer of their autonomy, which actually worsens the core anxiety loop.
4. **Functional Compromise:** By negotiating manageable task sizes, the partner receives support and the sufferer maintains their "Functional Identity."
